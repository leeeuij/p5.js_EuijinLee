Assignment 1 – Lost & Found

p5.js

I recreated my partner Ethan’s memory of his grandparents’ living room as a top-down floor plan using simple shapes, color, and animated fans. The sketch focuses on layout and atmosphere rather than realism, turning his text description into a clear spatial map.

Partner: Ethan Zhao

Sketch

Process & Reflection

Ethan described the room in plain and straightforward words, focusing on the layout and the main furniture. The description gave me a clear idea of what was in the room and where it was located, but it didn’t include exact sizes or measurements.

I imagined the room as if I was looking at it from above, like a floor plan. This way I could place the sofa against the wall, the table on the left with chairs around it, the cabinet and TV on the right side, and the fans where they were mentioned. I focused more on overall layout and balance than on small details. At first I tried a 3D perspective, but it felt confusing and less accurate. Switching to a flat plan view made the drawing match the description much better.

In the code, I represented the room step by step, using simple shapes to stand in for real objects. The floor is a large rectangle with darker lines suggesting wood planks. The table, chairs, cabinet, and TV are drawn with rect() in different sizes, while cups, snacks, and fan blades use ellipse(). I used fill() to give each object its own color so they are easy to tell apart. To add a sense of depth, I experimented with blendMode(MULTIPLY) for shadows and blendMode(SCREEN) for reflections, such as on the TV screen and window. I grouped objects and used translate(), push(), and pop() to move them without affecting everything else. The fans are the most dynamic part: their blades rotate each frame using frameCount, so they look like they are spinning.

There was a gap between my partner’s text and my first sketch. The description was clear, but my original 3D idea didn’t work well. After switching to the top-down view, the code version simplified many details but communicated the layout effectively. Overall, my interpretation of the space changed at each stage—from words, to drawing, to code.
